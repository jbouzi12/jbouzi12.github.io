The Rise of a Juggernaut

For this week's technical series, I will be exploring the rise of one of web development's most prominent languages; JavaScript. Ever since its inception in the mid 90's, JavaScript has become a top language due to its ease of use, and ability to deliver rich, dynamic web content. In 1995, Brendan Eich created JavaScript as a lightweight complement to Java (after many name changes, they decided to go with JavaScript in order to ride the rising the popularity of Java). However, no new language can survive let alone succeed by employing such marketing strategies; any new language must fulfill an emerging computing need.

For JavaScript, that need manifested in the form of Netscape Navigator, which was a dominant browser in the 90's in direct competition with Microsoft. JavaScript was its main language, providing dynamic front-end development. Since then, JavaScript has dramatically expanded into new domains such as mobile and cloud, accumulating millions of developers along the way. Although it has its issues (every language does), its rise to prominence can be attributed to its flexibility; JavaScript is easily learned and can be used to accomplish many things without much expertise on the language. This makes the case for JavaScript as a very strong language. As a result, companies such as Microsoft, Google, GM, Yahoo, Dow Jones, and Apple have all leveraged its vast potential to achieve a plethora of tasks.

101GreatGoals

This is one of the best sites for any soccer (or futbol) fanatic who want to get update news on their favorite teams, watch highlight videos of games from across the globe, and view funny posts from fans and players alike. Ironically, this site completely changed its layout just a week ago. I don't remember exactly what the previous layout was, so I'm just going to discuss this new one. Although it looks much more modern than its predecessor, I find the homepage to have too much content so close together. Mainly, the social feed right in the middle column as you scroll down is somewhat distracting; quickly glancing over the site you can easily mistaken twitter and instagram posts for site articles and the continuous stream of the social feed aids in this distraction. However, navigating away from the main page sees much improvement in the visual appeal of the site. Articles are accompanied by images, and at the top of each page there is a stream of the most recent content. This is a much better improvement from the previous site, which simply listed links to content (ex: game highlights) with a title and paragraph summary for each.
